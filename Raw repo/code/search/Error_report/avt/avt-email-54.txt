Hi Magnus,

One of the problems with the usual method of having only the receiver 
adjust its de-jitter buffer and play out times based on the arrival of 
traffic is that,

1.  In wireless systems like HSDPA/HRPD where a single down/forward link 
channel is shared among all the users in the cell and assigned by the 
scheduler to users based on their individual down/forward link quality, the 
E2E delays of packets can vary significantly at different locations in a 
cell and under different loading conditions (i.e., # users in the 
cell).  It is important to note that the variation in E2E (down/forward 
link) delays at different locations is the result of changes in the link 
throughput because of variations in the radio link quality.  The 
instantaneous data rate assigned to a user is based on what can be 
supported by the radio link at that location.  As this changes, so does the 
delivery time for packets of a particular size.

In the attached slide the dashed lines show the distribution of average E2E 
delays for packet-switched video telephony users being served in 57 cells 
by a HSDPA/HRPD-type of scheduler in each cell.  Note that in the 
simulations there are a significant # of users who experience an E2E delay 
of over 600ms.  The number of users seeing longer delays gets worse as the 
loading in the cells increases (i.e., going from 8 to 12 users/cell).

2.  To accommodate for these variations, a receiver can use a very deep 
(long) de-jitter buffer to accommodate for this significant variation in 
E2E delays.  However this produces a long static E2E delay as a static 
de-jitter buffer will have to accommodate for the longest E2E delay the 
receiver expects to receive.  In the cases we are seeing, the longest E2E 
delay can exceed what is acceptable for conversational services.

PDAR Message

The PDAR message allows terminals to still perform de-jitter buffer/play 
out time management based on packet arrivals.  But it also introduces a 
procedure to tell the sender the status of arrivals at the receiver.  We 
are establishing a control loop between the sender and receiver that allows 
the receiver to better control the play out times of the media (and not 
simply react to what it receives from the sender).

For example, as packets are starting to arrive delayed (i.e. closer to 
their play out times at the receiver) the receiver can start to delay its 
play out times and also indicate to the sender that packets are arriving 
later (and by how much) than the receiver requires.

I think there might be some confusion about the PDAR since we called it a 
"Request."  It does not request that packets be sent earlier by the sender 
since as you point out this is usually not possible.  It is an indication 
to the sender that the receiver is not receiving its packets at the desired 
time for play out (and by how much).  The sender is to interpret this 
indication to mean that the link rate may be too slow and/or congestion 
from cell loading too heavy to support the current rate it is transmitting 
at.

In summary, the PDAR is an indication of the receiver's play out situation 
as it is affected by the
+ link speed/quality
+ congestion
+ sender's packet sizes/sending rate

The sender can use the PDAR to adjust its output rate (essentially decrease 
its frame/packet sizes) to get its transmission through the slower link 
more quickly and address the play out situation at the receiver.  We think 
this is a good control-loop feedback variable since it is directly linked 
to the play out times of the receiver, and thus directly controls the 
quality rendered to the user.

The solid lines in the attached figure show the results of feeding back 
this packet delay information to the senders of point-to-point packet 
switched video telephony calls.  The senders adjust their transmitted video 
frame sizes (video QP values) based on this delay information and are able 
to adapt well to the variations in link and loading conditions.  Most of 
the E2E delays are centered around 150ms as opposed to the dashed lines 
where E2E delays peak around 620ms and can range up to 1000ms.

What are the other congestion indicators that you were thinking of?

One of the problems with the usual method of having only the receiver
adjust its de-jitter buffer and play out times based on the arrival of
traffic is that,<br><br>
1.&nbsp; In wireless systems like HSDPA/HRPD where a single down/forward
link channel is shared among all the users in the cell and assigned by
the scheduler to users based on their individual down/forward link
quality, the E2E delays of packets can vary significantly at different
locations in a cell and under different loading conditions (i.e., # users
in the cell).&nbsp; It is important to note that the variation in E2E
(down/forward link) delays at different locations is the result of
changes in the link throughput because of variations in the radio link
quality.&nbsp; The instantaneous data rate assigned to a user is based on
what can be supported by the radio link at that location.&nbsp; As this
changes, so does the delivery time for packets of a particular
size.<br><br>

E2E delays for packet-switched video telephony users being served in 57
cells by a HSDPA/HRPD-type of scheduler in each cell.&nbsp; Note that in

delay of over 600ms.&nbsp; The number of users seeing longer delays gets
worse as the loading in the cells increases (i.e., going from 8 to 12
users/cell).<br><br>

deep (long) de-jitter buffer to accommodate for this significant
variation in E2E delays.&nbsp; However this produces a long static E2E

longest E2E delay the receiver expects to receive.&nbsp; In the cases we

conversational services.<br><br>

PDAR Message<br><br>
The PDAR message allows terminals to still perform de-jitter buffer/play
out time management based on packet arrivals.&nbsp; But it also
introduces a procedure to tell the sender the status of arrivals at the
receiver.&nbsp; We are establishing a control loop between the sender and
receiver that allows the receiver to better control the play out times of
the media (and not simply react to what it receives from the
sender).<br><br>
For example, as packets are starting to arrive delayed (i.e. closer to
their play out times at the receiver) the receiver can start to delay its
play out times and also indicate to the sender that packets are arriving
later (and by how much) than the receiver requires.<br><br>
I think there might be some confusion about the PDAR since we called it a
&quot;Request.&quot;&nbsp; It does not request that packets be sent
earlier by the sender since as you point out this is usually not
possible.&nbsp; It is an indication to the sender that the receiver is
not receiving its packets at the desired time for play out (and by how
much).&nbsp; The sender is to interpret this indication to mean that the
link rate may be too slow and/or congestion from cell loading too heavy
to support the current rate it is transmitting at.&nbsp; <br><br>
In summary, the PDAR is an indication of the receiver's play out
situation as it is affected by the <br>
+ link speed/quality<br>
+ congestion<br>
+ sender's packet sizes/sending rate<br><br>
The sender can use the PDAR to adjust its output rate (essentially
decrease its frame/packet sizes) to get its transmission through the
slower link more quickly and address the play out situation at the
receiver.&nbsp; We think this is a good control-loop feedback variable
since it is directly linked to the play out times of the receiver, and
thus directly controls the quality rendered to the user.<br><br>
The solid lines in the attached figure show the results of feeding back
this packet delay information to the senders of point-to-point packet
switched video telephony calls.&nbsp; The senders adjust their
transmitted video frame sizes (video QP values) based on this delay
information and are able to adapt well to the variations in link and
loading conditions.&nbsp; Most of the E2E delays are centered around
150ms as opposed to the dashed lines where E2E delays peak around 620ms
and can range up to 1000ms.<br><br>

of?<br><br>
